<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p>
    The Time Blocking View is there to help you see your entire day laid out in order. It is still a list, but instead of being a general reminders list, it is a list of times of day with your reminder cards placed into those time slots. That makes it easier to understand not just what you need to do, but where each action lives within the flow of your day.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    Time blocking was created around the idea that many people stay more organized when their tasks are connected to actual time instead of floating around mentally or sitting in a generic list. Usually this kind of view is reserved for calendar events, but Lystaria takes the idea further by applying it to reminders as well. This gives you a stronger visual grasp of your day and helps turn your responsibilities into something that feels more concrete and easier to follow.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    This view is especially useful when you want a full-day perspective. It helps you notice how busy certain parts of the day are, how your reminders are spaced out, and whether your schedule feels realistic. Instead of guessing what your day looks like, you can scroll through it and see the structure directly.
  </p>
</div>

<GradientDivider />


<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>What The Time Blocking View is For</h2>
  <p>
    The Time Blocking View exists to give you a full visual of your day through a time-based list. It is not separate from your reminders system, but another way of viewing the same reminder data. The goal is to make your schedule easier to understand by showing reminders where they fall throughout the day instead of only showing them as part of a broader reminder feed.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    This helps when you need context. A normal list can show you what is due, but a time-based list shows you how those actions are distributed across the hours of your day. That makes it easier to stay on track, pace yourself, and understand whether your schedule feels balanced or overloaded.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    It also helps make planning feel more intentional. When you can see your reminder cards inside the actual times they belong to, your day becomes easier to navigate. That kind of visual order can reduce confusion and help you move through responsibilities with more clarity.
  </p>
</div>

<GradientDivider />

<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>How Reminders Appear</h2>
  <p>
    All of your reminders appear in the Time Blocking View inside their appropriate time slots. Each reminder card includes a default color strip so that the schedule is easier to scan visually. Those colors can be changed later using the small edit pencil, which gives you more control over how your day looks and how quickly you can recognize certain reminders.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    This matters because color can help make the layout feel less flat. When reminder cards are placed into a time-based list with visual color markers, it becomes much easier to glance through the day and understand the shape of it. That makes the system feel more organized and more readable.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    Since the cards are still reminders, they keep their identity here. This is not a separate event system pretending to be reminders. It is the reminders system shown through a time-blocked daily structure, which is what makes it so useful.
  </p>
</div>

<GradientDivider />

<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>What You Can and Cannot Do</h2>
  <p>
    You cannot create new reminders directly from the Time Blocking View. That part is intentional, because this page is meant to focus on seeing and working with the reminders that already exist rather than acting as the main creation screen. New reminders are still created from the regular reminders flow.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    Even so, this view is absolutely interactive. You can edit reminders here, and you can complete them here as well. That means you do not have to leave the Time Blocking View just to update or finish something, which helps keep the experience smooth and practical.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    The completion circle does not remain filled for recurring reminders, and that is also intentional. Recurring reminders need to become available again for the next occurrence, so they cannot behave like one-time tasks that stay permanently completed. This keeps the schedule accurate over time and allows recurring reminders to continue functioning properly.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>Why Time Blocking Is Helpful</h2>
  <p>
    Time blocking is helpful because it connects actions to actual parts of the day. For many people, this makes responsibilities feel easier to understand and easier to follow through on. Instead of holding a loose mental list of things that need to happen, you are able to see those things placed into the day itself.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    That can improve focus and organization in a very real way. When your reminders are attached to visible time slots, it becomes easier to judge what is realistic, what is crowded, and where you still have space. This can help prevent overloading yourself and can make the day feel more manageable.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    It also helps with staying on track. If you drift or lose focus, the Time Blocking View gives you a quick way to reconnect with the day. You can scroll through the timeline, see where you are, see what is coming next, and regain a sense of direction without having to piece everything together in your head.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>Navigating In</h2>
  <p>
    You can get to the Time Blocking View from the Reminders tab by tapping the alarm icon in the header. That icon acts as the entry point into this view, giving you a quick way to switch from the standard reminders page into the time-based layout of your day.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    This makes the feature easy to access without needing to dedicate a permanent button somewhere else on the screen. Since it lives in the header, it stays available while keeping the rest of the interface cleaner.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    Once you know where that icon is, getting into the Time Blocking View becomes second nature. It turns what might feel hidden at first into a seamless part of navigating the reminders system.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="docs-card">
 <h2>Navigating Out</h2>
  <p>
    To leave the Time Blocking View and return to the regular Reminders tab page, tap the date shown between the left and right white chevron icons. This is easy to forget at first because it is not the most obvious interaction, but it is part of what keeps the interface from becoming overloaded with extra buttons.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    Once you understand that the date acts as the way back, the navigation feels much more seamless. You can move into the Time Blocking View to get a full visual of the day, then tap the date to return to the broader reminders page when needed.
  </p>
  
  <DotDivider />

  <p>
    That design choice helps reduce clutter while still keeping the system smooth. Instead of stacking more and more obvious controls onto the page, it uses the space that is already there in a more intentional way.
  </p>
